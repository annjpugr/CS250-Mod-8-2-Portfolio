/* How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?

  As a developer, my goal is to create solutions that ‘answer’ to the needs of the customers and users, as described in the user-story. I do this by first reviewing the product backlog and the product
vision. By this point, I should have some structure in mind to implement a solution to the user story. If the story is too large, it should be broken down into manageable chunks such as one task per sprint 
and processed as such. I then develop an acceptance criterion to show when an output is ready. After reviewing the user-story and backlog, I relay my feedback to the PO and ask for clarification to further 
refine the development process as needed. Armed with enough data to create the deliverable, I develop a few test cases to lay out the framework of my proposed solution. Starting with the first test case, I 
start building the output that executes the first test case. I do this for all test cases until a final working product is created and meets the predetermined acceptance criteria.
  Having a user-story provides me with a context I can use to understand what the customers or users want in the final product. I use it as a guide to focus on the user-value when working on a task. It 
helps me facilitate testing by ensuring our output meets an acceptable criterion and because user-stories are broken down into smallest task units, it helps keep the job prioritized, estimatable, and 
deliverable in increments. 
*/

/* How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work? 

  When working with user stories, the goal is to keep tasks simple so that they are easily organized and achievable to reduce burn-out and bottlenecks. User stories are a baseline and can be modified to
the extent needed to achieve the best solution needed and valued by the user. I am personally leaning towards the application of Scrum Ban in my future development projects. Scrum Ban appeals to me in its 
minimalistic approach to organizing tasks, specifically combining sprint planning and visual work-in-progress. At the same time, Scrum Ban is also flexible because of the on-demand planning, the aim for 
continuous flow rather than strictly iterative delivery and planning the amount of work around capacity. 
*/

/* What does it mean to be a good team member in software development? 

  A great team member is someone that seeks to understand others and offers solutions to their problems as needed; they understand that people are not their problems. They are proactive and seek solutions
to complex challenges by breaking them down and addressing them individually. They are reliable in that you know they will deliver their tasks as expected and keep the team aware of their progress. They 
let you know if they are falling behind in their tasks to prevent team stagnation. They help others by sharing knowledge to avoid repetitive mistakes. They aim for quality work and maintaining standards. 
Great team members are self-aware and adjust their actions to the team goal accordingly. */
